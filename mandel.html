Statistical Engine & Methodology

This application utilizes the exact formulas designated in ISO 5725-2 to calculate between-laboratory and within-laboratory consistency statistics. Rather than using static lookup tables, this tool natively computes critical $t$ and $F$ distribution bounds using the `jStat` engine to support ANY combination of participating labs and replicates.

I. Mandel's h Statistic (Between-Lab Consistency)

The $h$ score evaluates whether a specific laboratory's average measurement is drifting significantly higher or lower than the grand consensus of all laboratories.

  • Formula: $h_i = (\bar{y}_i - \bar{\bar{y}}) / s_{\bar{y}}$
  • where $\bar{y}_i$ is the $i$-th laboratory's mean, $\bar{\bar{y}}$ is the grand mean of all $p$ laboratories, and $s_{\bar{y}}$ is the standard deviation of all laboratory means.
  • Critical Value ($h_{crit}$): Calculated via the inverse Student's t-distribution:
    $h_{crit} = \frac{(p-1) t}{\sqrt{p(p - 2 + t^2)}}$ *(where $t$ is the two-tailed critical value for $p-2$ degrees of freedom)*.
II. Mandel's k Statistic (Within-Lab Precision)

The $k$ score evaluates whether a specific laboratory's internal variance (its inability to repeat the exact same result) is statistically significantly worse than the aggregated precision of all other laboratories.

  • Formula: $k_i = s_i / s_r$
  • where $s_i$ is the standard deviation within the $i$-th laboratory (requires $n > 1$ replicates), and $s_r$ is the pooled within-laboratory standard deviation (square root of the average of all variances).
  • Critical Value ($k_{crit}$): Calculated via the inverse F-distribution:
    $k_{crit} = \sqrt{\frac{p}{1 + \frac{p-1}{F}}}$ *(where $F$ is the upper-tailed critical value for $\nu_1=n-1, \nu_2=(p-1)(n-1)$ degrees of freedom)*.
III. Significance Thresholds

The plotted threshold lines visually indicate the statistical boundaries for identifying outliers:

  • 1% Critical Limit (Red Line): A score breaching this line occurs by random chance less than 1% of the time ($\alpha = 0.01$). The laboratory is operating distinctly differently from the cohort and warrants immediate investigation (classic Outlier).
  • 5% Warning Limit (Yellow Line): A score breaching this line occurs by random chance less than 5% of the time ($\alpha = 0.05$). This is an early warning indicator requiring closer monitoring (popularly called a Straggler).

Interpreting Mandel's Plots

Mandel's $h$ and $k$ plots provide a fast, graphic overview of laboratory performance within a collaborative study. They are often used in tandem to paint a complete picture of an individual lab's capabilities.

1. The 'h' Plot (Systematic Averages)

The $h$ plot isolates systematic bias. It asks: "Is this lab consistently reporting higher or lower numbers than everyone else?"

  • Positive $h$ Values: The laboratory is reporting values higher than the consensus average.
  • Negative $h$ Values: The laboratory is reporting values lower than the consensus average.
  • Breaching thresholds: If lab $L3$ crosses the red 1% line in the positive direction, they have a statistically significant positive calibration bias.
2. The 'k' Plot (Internal Precision)

The $k$ plot isolates random error. It asks: "Is this lab heavily struggling to get the same answer twice?"

  • Only Positive Values: Variance is strictly positive, hence the $k$ statistic has a hard floor at zero. Values near zero indicate exceptional internal repeatability (the lab got the exact same number on all their replicate runs).
  • Values near 1.0: A $k$ value of exactly 1.0 means the lab's internal variation is perfectly matching the overall industry average.
  • Breaching thresholds: If lab $L5$ crosses the red 1% line, they possess severely worse repeatability than the other labs. Their testing procedure is likely chaotic or they are suffering from uncontrollable random contamination / instrument noise independent of their actual calibration.
3. Youden Plots vs. Mandel Plots

While Youden Plots require exactly two identical or highly similar samples (Sample A vs Sample B) to plot a 2D mapping of Systematic vs Random error across a cohort, Mandel's Statistics work by assessing multiple replicate measurements (Rep 1, Rep 2, Rep 3...) taken on a single sample.

Mandel's charts are practically superior when you have more than 2 strictly independent attempts at measuring a specific analyte. When using both tools, a lab that acts as an outlier on a Youden 45-degree axis (Systematic Error) will almost always breach the Mandel $h$ plot threshold for that specific sample level.
